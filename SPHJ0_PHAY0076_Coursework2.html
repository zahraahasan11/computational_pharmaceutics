#Q2 — Why is checking whether SMILES can be parsed an important part of molecular data cleaning?
#SMILES need to be checked because everything after this step needs a proper molecule. If the SMILES is broken or empty then RDKit gives nothing back and none of the properties like molecular weight or LogP can be calculated.
#Also broken SMILES usually means something went wrong when the data was entered or saved. If these bad ones are not caught early they will just cause errors later when trying to calculate descriptors or train the model.
#So basically it is like a quality check at the start to make sure only real usable molecules are being worked with.

from rdkit.Chem import Descriptors, rdMolDescriptors

def calc_descriptors(mol):
    return {
        'MolWt': Descriptors.MolWt(mol),
        'LogP': Descriptors.MolLogP(mol),
        'TPSA': Descriptors.TPSA(mol),
        'HBD': rdMolDescriptors.CalcNumHBD(mol),
        'HBA': rdMolDescriptors.CalcNumHBA(mol),
        'RotBonds': rdMolDescriptors.CalcNumRotatableBonds(mol),
        'AromaticRings': rdMolDescriptors.CalcNumAromaticRings(mol),
    }

#Q4 — Why might descriptors such as LogP, TPSA, and molecular weight be relevant when deciding on a formulation strategy?
#LogP tells us how soluble a drug is in water versus fat. In the data it goes from -1.73 to 5.76. Molecules with low LogP like those in the IR_tablet class dissolve well in gut fluids so a simple tablet is enough. Molecules with high LogP like those in the Lipid_system class do not dissolve in gut fluids so they need a lipid based system to keep them solubilised. Molecules in between like the ASD class have moderate LogP so they need an amorphous solid dispersion to improve their dissolution rate.
#TPSA measures the polar surface area of the molecule. It goes from 0 to 91.74. IR_tablet molecules have the highest TPSA meaning they are very polar and dissolve well in water. Lipid_system molecules have the lowest TPSA meaning they are non-polar and need lipid based help to get absorbed. So TPSA helps predict which direction the formulation should go.
#Molecular weight goes from 90.12 to 290.45. The lighter molecules tend to be the simple soluble ones that work as IR tablets. The heavier molecules tend to be the lipophilic ones that need lipid systems. This is because bigger molecules often have longer carbon chains which makes them less soluble and harder to absorb without formulation help.

#Q7 — What does the PCA plot show? What do PC1 and PC2 appear to represent in terms of molecular properties?
#The PCA plot shows the 59 molecules reduced down to two dimensions. Each dot is a molecule and the colour shows its formulation class. PC1 explains 45.3% of the total variance and PC2 explains 25.2% so together they capture 70.5% of the information in the original 7 descriptors.
#PC1 is the horizontal axis and captures the most variance. From the loadings table LogP has the highest positive loading on PC1 at 0.536 while TPSA and HBD have the strongest negative loadings at -0.454 and -0.431. So PC1 represents lipophilicity versus polarity. The green dots (IR_tablet) sit on the left because they are polar with high TPSA. The blue dots (Lipid_system) sit on the right because they are lipophilic with high LogP. The orange dots (ASD) sit in the middle.
#PC2 is the vertical axis and captures less variance. From the loadings table MolWt has the highest loading on PC2 at 0.621 followed by HBA at 0.509 and RotBonds at 0.426. So PC2 represents molecular size. Larger heavier molecules with more hydrogen bond acceptors sit higher up on the plot while smaller lighter ones sit lower down.
#The main finding is that the three formulation classes separate mainly along PC1 confirming that lipophilicity is the most important property for deciding which formulation a molecule needs.
